# Trading Psychology

Synthesized from: Livermore (Reminiscences), Douglas (Trading in the Zone), Schwager (Market Wizards interviews),
Minervini, O'Neil.
The biggest edge in trading is not a better indicator. It is better mental discipline.
Most traders lose not because their systems are wrong but because they cannot execute them correctly.

---

## The Market Is Always Right (Livermore)

1. The market does not care what you paid. It does not care what you think. It does not owe you a recovery.
2. When price is telling you something, believe price — not your thesis. If a stock is falling on good news, something is wrong that you do not know yet.
3. Your opinion is not a reason to hold a losing position. The trade was either wrong or early. Both require the same response: cut it.

## Consistency Over Cleverness (Douglas)

4. A random outcome on any single trade does not invalidate a positive-expectancy system. You must execute every valid signal to realize the edge over time.
5. Skipping signals because you "feel bad about this one" destroys statistical edge faster than market conditions do.
6. The goal is not to be right on this trade. The goal is to execute the system correctly on every trade so that the edge compounds.

## Cognitive Biases That Kill Accounts

7. **Loss aversion**: losses feel twice as painful as gains feel good (Kahneman). This causes traders to cut winners too early and hold losers too long — the exact opposite of correct behavior.
8. **Confirmation bias**: after entering a trade, you selectively notice news that confirms the thesis and ignore news that challenges it. Actively look for the bearish case after you enter.
9. **Overconfidence after a winning streak**: a series of wins creates the illusion of mastery. Position sizes blow up right before a market shift.
10. **Anchoring**: fixating on your entry price as a reference point. The market has no idea where you got in. Price your exit decisions from current price, not your cost basis.
11. **Recency bias**: assuming recent market behavior will continue. Uptrends do not last forever. Corrections end too.

## The Discipline of the Pre-Set Plan (O'Neil, Minervini)

12. Write down the entry price, stop price, and target price before placing the trade. Log it. This creates accountability.
13. If you cannot articulate why you are buying in one sentence, you should not buy.
14. Never change your stop after entry — only trail it in your favor. Moving a stop down to "give it more room" is how small losses become catastrophic ones.
15. The trading plan is made when you are calm. Execution happens when you may not be calm. Trust the plan.

## After a Loss (Livermore's hard-learned rules)

16. After a losing trade, wait. Do not immediately look for the next entry to "make it back." The market does not award compensation for prior losses.
17. Review the loss objectively: did you follow the system, or did you deviate? If you followed the system, it is a cost of doing business. If you deviated, that is the problem.
18. Paper trading bad habits away takes the same number of repetitions as trading them away with real money. Treat paper trades with the same seriousness.

## Process vs. Outcome (Schwager's Wizards)

19. A good process with a bad outcome is still a good process. A bad process with a good outcome (luck) is still a bad process that will eventually destroy you.
20. Judge your performance by whether you followed your rules, not by whether the trade was profitable. A 1R loss from a correctly executed setup is a success. A 3R loss from ignoring your stop is a failure even if it eventually comes back.
21. Keep a brief trade journal: entry reason, exit reason, and what you learned. Even three sentences per trade compounds into enormous insight over a year.

## When to Stop Trading (Druckenmiller, O'Neil)

22. If you are down more than 3% for the day, stop. The kill switch enforces this automatically in our system.
23. If you have had three consecutive losing weeks, reduce size to 50% and review your setup quality.
24. If the market is in correction or bear mode, going to cash is not cowardice. It is discipline. The best traders sit in cash for weeks at a time when conditions are wrong.
25. You cannot force the market to give you opportunities. When there are no A-grade setups, there are no trades. Patience is the hardest skill and the most valuable one.
